# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test (Version 4)

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates the PROGOS English Speaking Test as (1) an assessment instrument, (2) a reporting system, and (3) a tool for organisational decision-making.

The review is based solely on materials examined during this project:
- screenshots of test items and instructions;
- candidate score reports;
- extracted report text and comments;
- publicly available PROGOS information;
- PROGOS terms and privacy-management documents.

No assumptions are made regarding unpublished technical documentation, internal scoring algorithms, or psychometric studies that were not reviewed.

### Summary of Findings

| Area | Evaluation |
|---|---|
| Practicality | Strong |
| Authenticity | Moderate to Strong |
| Measurement of Spoken Production | Stronger |
| Measurement of Spoken Interaction | Limited |
| Diagnostic Usefulness | Limited |
| Suitability for Progress Measurement | Weak |
| Suitability for Course Design | Weak |
| Report Individualisation | Weak |

The principal conclusion is that PROGOS may be useful as a supplementary external benchmark providing a broad indication of speaking proficiency. It should not replace the organisation's existing assessment system for placement, pre/post assessment, course design, or instructional decision-making.

---

# 1. Purpose and Intended Use

## Observed Purpose

The reviewed materials indicate that PROGOS is intended to:

- provide a broad indication of English speaking proficiency;
- report a CEFR-aligned overall level;
- provide six analytic category scores.

## Appropriate Uses

- broad external benchmarking;
- supplementary proficiency information;
- broad placement support.

## Inappropriate Uses

- fine-grained placement;
- measurement of short-term learning gains;
- diagnostic assessment;
- course design;
- teacher assignment.

---

# 2. Test Methodology

## Test Structure

1. Interview responses.
2. Reading aloud.
3. Presentation.
4. Graphics presentation.
5. Communication activity.

## Task Evaluation Summary

| Task | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| Interview | Spontaneous speech | Insufficient evidence available for detailed review |
| Reading Aloud | Standardisation, pronunciation evidence | Limited communicative value; some unnatural items |
| Presentation | Sustained production, coherence | May favour experienced professionals |
| Graphics Presentation | Business authenticity, discourse organisation | Also measures graph literacy |
| Communication Activity | Controlled response task | Does not provide genuine interaction |

### Key Observation

The test appears considerably stronger as a measure of spoken production than spoken interaction.

---

# 3. Validity

## Content Validity

The test samples several aspects of spoken production through multiple task types.

## Construct Validity

The principal concern is Interaction.

The test provides limited opportunities to demonstrate:

- negotiation of meaning;
- repair;
- conversational initiative;
- turn-taking;
- co-construction of meaning.

Consequently, the Interaction score appears to be supported by less direct evidence than other reported categories.

## Authenticity

Most tasks are reasonably authentic and workplace relevant.

The Reading Aloud task is substantially less authentic.

## Interpretive Validity

Several report comments appear broader than the evidence generated by the tasks themselves.

## Consequential Validity

There is a risk that stakeholders may:

- overinterpret unchanged scores;
- underestimate genuine learning gains;
- treat generic feedback as individual diagnosis.

---

# 4. Reliability

The design supports consistency of administration through:

- identical prompts;
- standardised timing;
- automated delivery.

However, the reviewed materials do not permit conclusions regarding:

- score stability;
- measurement error;
- equivalence of forms;
- reliability of subscores.

Accordingly, no conclusion is reached regarding overall score reliability.

---

# 5. Test Instructions and Language

Language-testing literature generally recommends that instructions:

- be clear and unambiguous;
- minimise construct-irrelevant difficulty;
- avoid imposing unnecessary linguistic demands beyond the target construct.

Potential concerns identified in the reviewed materials include:

- English-only instructions;
- audio-only instructions in some sections;
- additional listening and processing demands.

For lower-level learners, instruction language may therefore increase cognitive load and introduce additional demands unrelated to speaking ability itself.

---

# 6. Fairness and Accessibility

## Strengths

- standardised administration;
- generally familiar topics;
- relatively low cultural bias.

## Concerns

Some tasks assume:

- workplace experience;
- familiarity with professional contexts.

This may disadvantage:

- younger candidates;
- students;
- test takers with limited professional experience.

---

# 7. Practicality

Practicality is one of PROGOS's strongest features.

Strengths include:

- automated administration;
- scalability;
- rapid reporting;
- ease of deployment;
- consistent administration.

---

# 8. Evaluation of the Score Reports

## Report Structure

The reports are visually clear and easy to understand.

## Overall Assessment Comment

The Overall Assessment comments focus primarily on vocabulary and expressions and do not fully reflect all six analytic categories.

## Interaction Comments

The reviewed reports include comments referring to following another speaker's lead in conversation.

The test itself provides limited opportunities to demonstrate:

- leading conversations;
- negotiation of meaning;
- repair;
- turn management.

Accordingly, the relationship between the report comments and the evidence generated by the test is unclear.

## Diagnostic Usefulness

The reports:

- do not cite evidence from individual performances;
- provide limited diagnostic information;
- provide largely generic recommendations.

## Evidence of Templating

Comparison of reports suggests that feedback is largely level-based and templated.

The reports therefore appear to provide:

> level-based learning guidance

rather than:

> individual diagnostic feedback.

---

# 9. Legal and Ethical Considerations

The reviewed documents provide general information regarding personal information handling and service use.

From an assessment-governance perspective, the reviewed materials provide limited specific information regarding:

- retention of voice recordings;
- handling of speech data;
- score review procedures.

No conclusions regarding legal compliance are made in this report.

---

# 10. Suitability for the Organisation's Purposes

The organisation's internal assessment:

- assesses six dimensions;
- uses a 1.0–9.0 scale with 0.25 increments;
- supports placement, progress measurement, and course design.

## Student Class Allocation

PROGOS may provide supplementary information regarding broad proficiency.

It should not replace the internal assessment.

## Pre- and Post-Training Assessment

Not recommended.

The issue is not that the test is automated or CEFR-aligned. The issue is that broad proficiency bands are not sufficiently sensitive for measuring the relatively small gains typically achieved in corporate training programmes.

Meaningful improvement may therefore occur without any change in reported level.

## Course Design and Teacher Assignment

Not recommended.

The reports do not provide sufficient individual diagnostic information to support:

- syllabus design;
- teacher selection;
- targeted interventions.

---

# 11. Recommendations

| Purpose | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Broad external benchmarking | Possible supplementary use |
| Initial broad placement | Possible supplementary use |
| Fine-grained placement | Not recommended |
| Pre/post-training assessment | Not recommended |
| Course design | Not recommended |
| Teacher assignment | Not recommended |
| Diagnostic assessment | Not recommended |

---

# Final Judgement

PROGOS is a practical, scalable automated speaking test that provides a broad indication of speaking proficiency.

Its principal limitation for this organisation is not that it is automated or CEFR-aligned. Rather, the information produced by the test is insufficiently sensitive and insufficiently diagnostic for the purposes for which the organisation uses its internal assessment system.

Accordingly, PROGOS should not replace the organisation's existing speaking and listening assessment practices and should only be considered, if at all, as a supplementary external benchmark.
