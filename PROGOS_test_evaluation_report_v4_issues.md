# Issues Identified in the PROGOS Test Evaluation Report, Version 4

## Overall Assessment

`PROGOS_test_evaluation_report_v4.md` has a sensible outline, but it is not yet a defensible evaluation report. It reads more like a position paper containing conclusions without the supporting analysis.

## Major Problems

### 1. No Documented Review Methodology

- No evaluation framework, research questions, review procedure, rating criteria, or definition of terms such as "Strong" and "Weak."
- No explanation of who reviewed the evidence or how conclusions were reached.

### 2. The Evidence Base Is Not Traceable

- Lines 7–12 mention screenshots, reports, public information, and legal documents without identifying filenames, URLs, dates, versions, or sample sizes.
- Findings do not cite particular test items, screenshots, report passages, or source pages.

### 3. Conclusions Exceed the Available Evidence

- "Relatively low cultural bias" at line 168 cannot be established from a small set of screenshots.
- "Authenticity: Moderate to Strong" and similar summary ratings have no defined rubric.
- The report recommends possible benchmarking despite explicitly concluding that score reliability cannot be assessed.

### 4. Test Methodology Is Barely Described

Lines 61–77 only name five task types. The report does not document:

- the number of items;
- timing and preparation time;
- response format;
- instructions;
- scoring categories;
- aggregation of scores;
- test forms;
- automated scoring processes;
- administration conditions.

"Interview" may be misleading if candidates do not interact with an interviewer.

### 5. The Validity Analysis Is Too Shallow

- There is no explicit definition of the speaking construct.
- There is no systematic mapping between tasks, claimed abilities, scoring categories, and report interpretations.
- Validity is treated as a general property of the test rather than an argument concerning particular score interpretations and uses.

### 6. Reliability Is Confused with Standardisation

- Identical prompts and automated delivery support administrative consistency, but do not demonstrate score reliability.
- No evidence is provided concerning test–retest stability, scoring consistency, form equivalence, classification accuracy, measurement error, or subscore reliability.
- Reusing identical prompts could introduce familiarity and test-security concerns.

### 7. Existing CEFR Analysis Has Not Been Incorporated

The project already established that:

- Range and Interaction do not adequately match the official CEFR descriptor for the reported competence and level;
- Accuracy, Fluency, Coherence, and Phonology are closer matches.

Version 4 does not present this evidence, the official descriptors, workbook references, or comparison tables. It also does not explain that the Overall Assessment is not itself a CEFR competence.

### 8. The Score-Report Evaluation Is Underdeveloped

- Statements about templating and generic feedback do not identify how many reports were compared.
- There are no quoted examples or side-by-side comparisons.
- All six analytic categories are not examined individually.
- The meaning of ratings such as "A2 High" and "B2 and above" is not analysed.
- The method used to derive the overall level from analytic results is not discussed.

### 9. The Organisational Comparison Is Not Validly Established

- Lines 260–264 describe the internal assessment but do not evaluate its validity or reliability.
- A scale using 0.25 increments is not necessarily more sensitive merely because it reports finer numbers.
- PROGOS and the internal test need to be compared by construct, tasks, scoring, reliability, intended use, and decision thresholds.

### 10. The Pre/Post-Testing Conclusion Needs Empirical Support

- Broad CEFR categories may be insensitive to small gains, but this alone does not establish that PROGOS cannot measure progress.
- The report needs information about all available scores, measurement error, test–retest reliability, alternate forms, and the minimum detectable change.

### 11. Fairness and Accessibility Coverage Is Incomplete

- There is no treatment of disability accommodations, hearing conditions, microphone quality, speech-recognition bias, accent variation, digital access, or technical failures.
- Workplace familiarity may be relevant to the intended domain rather than automatically constituting unfairness; the target population must first be defined.

### 12. Practicality Is Asserted Rather Than Evaluated

The report does not address:

- costs;
- test duration;
- technical requirements;
- support arrangements;
- retest policy;
- failure handling;
- procurement burden;
- implementation risks.

### 13. Legal and Ethical Analysis Is Only a Placeholder

- No documents, clauses, jurisdictions, or policy dates are cited.
- Missing topics include retention periods, processors, international transfers, consent, automated decision-making, appeal procedures, access rights, and deletion requests.

### 14. Recommendations Are Not Actionable

The report says what should not be done but does not specify:

- evidence to request from PROGOS;
- questions for the vendor;
- a local validation study;
- pilot design;
- acceptable decision thresholds;
- conditions under which use might become appropriate.

### 15. Structural and Editorial Weaknesses

- The title and every numbered section use level-one headings.
- "Strong," "Stronger," "Limited," and "Weak" mix comparative and absolute judgements.
- There is no references section, evidence appendix, item-level review, report-comparison appendix, glossary, or dedicated limitations section.

## Conclusion

The report's principal concerns—particularly interactional competence, diagnostic usefulness, and sensitivity for short-term progress—are plausible. However, Version 4 does not provide enough traceable evidence to support its stronger conclusions or recommendations.

The next version should convert each assertion into a documented claim supported by specific test items, report extracts, CEFR comparisons, source citations, and clearly stated limitations.
