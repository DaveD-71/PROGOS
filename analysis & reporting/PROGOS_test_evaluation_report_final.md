# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test

Prepared: 2026-07-07

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates PROGOS as:

- a speaking test
- a score-reporting system
- a possible tool for organisational decision-making

The main conclusion is cautious. PROGOS appears usable as a broad, standardised external speaking benchmark, but the evidence now available in this repository does not justify using it as a primary tool for fine-grained placement, short-cycle progress measurement, course design, teacher assignment, or individual diagnostic feedback.

The second Aozawa administration on 2026-07-06 adds useful reliability evidence, but it does not resolve the reliability question. The 2026-07-01 and 2026-07-06 administrations produced a large apparent score change over five days: overall level moved from `A2 High` to `B1 High`, with several analytic subscores also changing. However, the forms were not identical, the Part 5 content changed, and the 2026-07-06 administration included an intervention by Dave during the Part 5 preparation period. Therefore, the two administrations are best treated as evidence of interpretive instability and comparability risk, not as a controlled test-retest reliability study.

The strongest observed evidence from the test concerns spoken production: short answers, read-aloud performance, prepared presentation, graph description, and timed responses. The weakest direct evidence concerns live interaction. The so-called Communication Activity uses fixed prerecorded prompts, so it cannot show full co-constructed interaction, clarification, repair, turn management, or adaptive response to a live interlocutor.

The score reports are readable and motivating, but their diagnostic value is limited. They do not cite candidate utterances, do not explain how observed responses led to ratings, and do not flag major task-level problems. In Aozawa's first test, he misunderstood the entire Part 5 role and repeatedly answered as an employer or recruiter rather than advising a job applicant. The report did not identify that issue. In the second test, Part 5 was completed more appropriately, but the intervention during preparation means the result is not directly comparable to a fully standard administration.

The public legal pages clarify useful operational and data-handling points, including recording, outsourcing, international handling, result timing, proprietary scoring criteria, retest rules, and restrictions on recording or disclosing test content. They do not provide the psychometric, scoring-method, reliability, form-equivalence, measurement-error, or AI-governance evidence needed to support high-stakes or fine-grained use.

Recommended use: supplementary external benchmark only.

Not recommended as primary evidence for:

- fine-grained placement
- short-cycle pre/post progress measurement
- course design
- teacher assignment
- individual diagnostic planning

## Test Purpose and Observed Design

### Observed purpose

The reports present PROGOS as a business English speaking test that reports:

- a CEFR-aligned overall level
- six analytic category ratings
- learner-facing feedback and study advice

The user-policy page states that PROGOS uses automated voice delivery and scores results according to the company's own criteria.

### Observed test structure

The observed test parts are:

1. Interview
2. Reading Aloud
3. Presentation
4. Graphics Presentation
5. Communication Activity

Observed timing:

| Part | Observed format | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Part 1 - Interview | 10 audio questions | 20 seconds speaking time per question |
| Part 2 - Reading Aloud | 8 displayed sentences | 10 seconds per sentence |
| Part 3 - Presentation | one topic prompt | 40 seconds preparation, 60 seconds speaking |
| Part 4 - Graphics Presentation | graph description | 40 seconds preparation, 60 seconds speaking |
| Part 5 - Communication Activity | scenario plus four fixed audio prompts | 40 seconds preparation, then 30 seconds after each prompt |

### Construct coverage

The task mix samples useful speaking evidence:

- short prompted answers
- read-aloud pronunciation and prosody
- prepared monologic presentation
- visual-information description
- timed response to audio prompts

It samples less direct evidence of:

- negotiation of meaning
- clarification and repair in real time
- turn-taking management
- initiating, redirecting, or jointly developing a conversation
- adapting to an unpredictable interlocutor

This distinction matters because Interaction is one of the reported analytic categories.

## Task and Construct Analysis

### Part 1 - Interview

Part 1 elicits short spontaneous responses across familiar and workplace-related topics. It is useful for sampling short-answer production under time pressure.

It is not a live interview in the ordinary sense. The candidate responds to fixed audio questions, and the interlocutor does not react, clarify, or follow up.

The first Aozawa form included item-quality concerns already documented in the retention file. The clearest example is `How do you imagine your company this time next year?`, which is understandable but under-specified and non-idiomatic. A more natural version would be `What do you imagine your company will be like this time next year?`

The second Aozawa form used different Part 1 questions, including household, manager, customer, sales, dismissal, employee-retention, and health-care questions. The form therefore changed materially between the two administrations.

### Part 2 - Reading Aloud

Part 2 gives controlled evidence of:

- decoding
- pronunciation
- stress and rhythm
- sentence-level fluency

It gives little direct evidence of Range, Coherence, or Interaction because the candidate is reading fixed text rather than generating language.

The first Aozawa form contained several wording concerns, including `request for changes`, `shift our production to somewhere in Southeast Asia`, `Information Tech company`, and the contextually odd `their existing products ... on Thursday`.

The second form also contained variable lexical and syntactic demands. Examples include:

- `An advantageous career move is what you need, don't you think?`
- `So, what's stopping them from making a decision and pursuing these options?`
- `She's focusing too much on what she's getting out of this networking event.`

Those sentences may be valid read-aloud material, but they are not equal in difficulty to simple workplace sentences. Without item-calibration evidence, this variation remains an interpretation risk.

### Part 3 - Presentation

Part 3 is a strong task for sustained production. It gives useful evidence for:

- Range
- Accuracy
- Fluency
- Coherence
- topic development

The two observed Aozawa forms used related but not identical prompts:

| Date | Prompt |
|---|---|
| 2026-07-01 | Talk about the best colleague you ever worked with. |
| 2026-07-06 | Talk about the best company you worked for. |

Both are workplace-relevant. They may favour candidates with work experience and may be less suitable for candidates without professional experience unless alternative study/community examples are allowed.

### Part 4 - Graphics Presentation

Part 4 is one of the strongest business-relevant tasks because it elicits:

- describing trends
- comparing quantities
- selecting relevant information
- organising a short explanation
- using numerical and graph language

It also introduces graph-literacy and time-constrained interpretation demands. These are defensible in a business-English test, but they mean performance is not purely speaking ability.

The two Aozawa forms used different graph tasks:

| Date | Topic |
|---|---|
| 2026-07-01 | advertising-industry revenue in Country A and Country B, 2013-2017 |
| 2026-07-06 | annual passengers using metro systems in Country G and Country H, 2014-2018 |

The task type is stable, but the content is not identical. This limits direct test-retest interpretation.

### Part 5 - Communication Activity

Part 5 is the main construct-validity concern.

The topic and communicative functions are realistic, but the format is not live interaction. The candidate hears fixed prerecorded prompts and responds into fixed time windows. The candidate cannot:

- negotiate meaning
- ask for clarification and receive a contingent answer
- repair a shared misunderstanding
- manage turns
- initiate a topic
- influence the direction of the exchange

Therefore, Part 5 supports evidence about prompted responses in a simulated conversation. It does not strongly support CEFR-style oral interaction as a co-constructed activity.

### Aozawa 0701 Part 5 misunderstanding

In the 2026-07-01 administration, Part 5 was about advising someone preparing for a job interview. Aozawa persistently answered as if he were an employer or recruiter:

- he described `my company` and its departments rather than documents to bring
- he discussed workplace dress rules and his own T-shirt rather than interview clothing
- he answered salary and holiday questions as employment-policy questions rather than as interview-advice questions
- he invited the speaker to apply and contact him

This was not a single weak answer. It was a role/scenario misunderstanding across all four prompts. That confounds interpretation of the intended Communication Activity.

### Aozawa 0706 Part 5 intervention

In the 2026-07-06 administration, Part 5 was about employee training. During the preparation period, Dave intervened and explained possible interpretations of `training attended`. This is direct evidence that the administration was not fully standardised.

The intervention likely helped clarify the scenario, and Aozawa then responded more directly to the prompts. That is useful evidence about the role of task comprehension, but it limits comparability with a standard administration.

The contrast between the two administrations supports a key conclusion: Part 5 performance can be materially affected by task and role comprehension. If the intended construct is spoken production, clearer task setup or L1 support would reduce construct-irrelevant difficulty. If the intended construct includes English-only comprehension of task instructions, then the report should make that interpretation explicit.

## CEFR Alignment and Score-Report Analysis

### Important scope point

The Overall Assessment summary in each report is not itself a CEFR competence in the descriptor workbook. It should not be treated as a direct CEFR descriptor match.

CEFR workbook matching applies only to the six individual reported competences:

- Range
- Accuracy
- Fluency
- Interaction
- Coherence
- Phonology

### Aozawa 0701 CEFR comparison

The 2026-07-01 Aozawa report gave:

| Category | Reported level | Same-competence same-level CEFR match? | Judgment |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Range | B1 | No | PROGOS wording is stronger than official B1 General linguistic range. |
| Accuracy | A2 | Yes | Acceptable paraphrase of A2 Grammatical accuracy. |
| Fluency | B2 and above | Yes | Acceptable match to the selected Fluency descriptor, though the open-ended label is not explained. |
| Interaction | A2 | No | Wording is closer to A2+ than A2, and the task does not directly support conversation leadership/following. |
| Coherence | B1 | Yes | Close paraphrase of B1 Coherence and cohesion. |
| Phonology | B1 | Yes | Close match to B1 Overall phonological control. |

### Aozawa 0706 CEFR comparison

The 2026-07-06 Aozawa report gave:

| Category | Reported level | Same-competence same-level CEFR match? | Judgment |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Range | B2 and above | No | Using B2+ as the proxy, the report wording is closer to B2 General linguistic range than B2+. |
| Accuracy | B1 | No | The reference to noticeable first-language influence is closer to B1+ Grammatical accuracy than B1. |
| Fluency | B1 | Yes | Acceptable match to B1 Fluency. |
| Interaction | B1 | Yes | Close wording match to B1 Overall oral interaction. |
| Coherence | B2 and above | No | Using B2+ as the proxy, the phrase `limited number` is closer to B2 Coherence and cohesion. |
| Phonology | B1 | Yes | Close match to B1 Overall phonological control. |

The 0706 findings add a new problem. Several report comments align more closely with a neighbouring CEFR descriptor than with the reported level/proxy level. This does not prove the score is wrong, but it weakens the learner-facing interpretation that the category comment is a precise explanation of the reported level.

### Overall Assessment summaries

The 0701 Overall Assessment is not a useful synthesis of overall speaking performance. It mainly concerns simple words, phrases, sentences, and basic vocabulary/expressions. It omits substantive information about Accuracy, Fluency, Coherence, and Phonology and gives only a limited interactional statement.

The 0706 Overall Assessment is more performance-like because it refers to presentation and social conversation. It says the candidate can give a reasonably smooth presentation about social issues of interest and continue a social conversation with detailed explanations. However, this still overreaches the observed evidence in two ways:

- the observed Part 5 is not a live social conversation
- the report does not show how the statement was derived from the candidate's actual responses

Both summaries use unqualified `You can` statements. They present broad ability inferences as facts rather than as interpretations based on a limited task sample.

### Diagnostic usefulness and individualisation

The reports are clear and potentially motivating. They are weak as individual diagnostic reports because they do not:

- cite candidate utterances
- identify task-specific successes or errors
- explain why a rating was awarded
- connect advice to observed performance
- flag major scenario or role misunderstandings

The comparison among Aozawa 0701, Aozawa 0706, and Saki 0630 supports a category-by-level templating interpretation. When the same category and level occur, the comment/advice/example content appears substantively reusable. When the level changes, the text changes. This does not invalidate the scores, but it means the learner-facing feedback should be treated as level-based guidance rather than individual diagnosis.

Examples of advice that is not demonstrably anchored to observed performance include:

- practising `I wish`
- using `Hmm` to express doubt or disagreement
- opening opinions with `In my opinion`
- using `basically` to avoid silence
- practising `hence`
- watching or repeating public speeches

These may be useful study suggestions, but the reports do not show that they were selected because of anything the candidate actually did.

## Reliability and Interpretation Limits

### What standardisation supports

The observed design supports administrative consistency in several respects:

- fixed prompts
- fixed timings
- automated audio delivery
- standard report format
- repeatable task structure across administrations

Those features support standardisation. They do not establish score reliability.

### What remains unavailable

The repo does not contain evidence about:

- test-retest stability
- form equivalence
- measurement error
- classification consistency
- subscore reliability
- agreement between automated scores and qualified human ratings
- how analytic ratings are weighted or aggregated
- how the overall level is derived from the six category ratings

### Aozawa 0701 vs 0706 score comparison

The two Aozawa administrations are useful because they provide two reports for the same candidate within five days.

| Category | 2026-07-01 | 2026-07-06 | Observed change |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Overall | A2 High | B1 High | up one reported overall band |
| Range | B1 | B2 and above | up substantially |
| Accuracy | A2 | B1 | up |
| Fluency | B2 and above | B1 | down |
| Interaction | A2 | B1 | up |
| Coherence | B1 | B2 and above | up substantially |
| Phonology | B1 | B1 | unchanged |

This pattern is not easy to interpret as a simple, stable proficiency estimate. Most categories moved upward, one moved downward, and one stayed unchanged over a very short interval.

The change may reflect some combination of:

- real performance variation between administrations
- different task forms
- different prompt difficulty
- greater familiarity with the test format
- different topic fit
- reduced misunderstanding in the second Part 5
- Dave's intervention in the second Part 5 preparation period
- normal measurement variation

The available evidence cannot separate these causes.

### Reliability interpretation

The 0701/0706 comparison does not prove that PROGOS is unreliable. It is not a controlled reliability study.

It does show that the reported score profile can change substantially between two close administrations under conditions that are not transparently explained by the report. That is a material limitation for organisational use, especially for pre/post testing or fine placement.

If a candidate can move from `A2 High` to `B1 High` within five days, stakeholders need guidance on whether that change is:

- expected normal variation
- a meaningful proficiency change
- a form/content effect
- an administration effect
- a scoring/classification effect

The public materials reviewed do not provide that guidance.

### Part 5 and reliability

The two administrations strengthen the earlier concern about Part 5. In 0701, task misunderstanding dominated the Communication Activity. In 0706, an external clarification occurred during preparation, and the response was more aligned with the intended topic.

This is important for reliability because it shows that score-relevant performance may depend heavily on whether the candidate understands the scenario and role. The report does not disclose whether or how such comprehension problems are detected, discounted, or incorporated into Interaction, Coherence, or overall scoring.

## Fairness, Accessibility, and Practicality

### Fairness and accessibility

Observed strengths:

- standardised delivery
- short administration window
- familiar workplace-oriented domains
- reduced live-examiner variation

Observed concerns:

- workplace prompts may advantage candidates with office or business experience
- Part 1 and Part 5 depend on audio-only prompt comprehension
- Part 4 adds graph-literacy and rapid visual-interpretation demands
- Part 5 role comprehension can materially affect performance
- English-only task setup may introduce construct-irrelevant difficulty if the intended construct is spoken production rather than English-only task comprehension

The available evidence does not support a strong judgment on:

- disability accommodations
- accent bias
- microphone or recording-quality effects
- speech-recognition bias
- technical failure rates

These areas remain unevaluated rather than satisfactory.

### Practicality

PROGOS appears practically attractive:

- it is short
- it is standardised
- it does not require a live examiner
- reports are generated through an existing platform
- the public terms state that results are posted within five business days

Practicality should be separated from interpretive quality. A test can be operationally efficient while still requiring caution for fine-grained or consequential decisions.

## Legal and Data-Governance Observations

This section is descriptive, not legal advice.

The current public pages are materially more informative than the older corporate privacy-management page. They identify voice data, possible monitoring-camera images, employer or service-provider disclosure in applicable contracts, outsourcing and re-outsourcing, international handling including Japan and the Philippines, and data-subject rights.

### What the public pages clearly state

The captured public pages state or indicate that:

- PROGOS uses automated voice delivery
- scores are based on the company's own evaluation criteria
- results are posted within five business days
- inquiries about test results or test content will not be answered
- no representation or warranty is made regarding the completeness or accuracy of results
- audio is recorded during the test
- monitored tests may involve PC-camera image data
- personal data include name, email address, voice data, IP address, cookie information, and website access history
- personal data may be handled in multiple countries, including Japan and the Philippines
- outsourcing and re-outsourcing are permitted
- anonymised statistical data may be used for future product/service development and sales activity
- personal data are retained unless deletion is requested, and deletion after request is stated to occur within one month
- technical interruptions have different rules depending on monitored/unmonitored administration
- re-examination after communication problems or applicant-attributable recording defects may require a new application and fee
- recording test content, taking screenshots, and disclosing questions are prohibited by the test-taker terms
- content and terms may be changed with notice
- Japanese governs where versions conflict

### What remains unclear or unavailable

The public pages reviewed do not provide:

- psychometric validation evidence
- a scoring manual
- task-to-score mappings
- score weighting or aggregation rules
- test-retest reliability evidence
- form-equivalence evidence
- subscore reliability evidence
- classification-consistency evidence
- measurement-error information
- an explicit routine retention period for voice data absent a deletion request
- an explicit routine retention period for monitoring images absent a deletion request
- a clear score-appeal or human-review procedure
- AI-model governance detail for the scoring process

For assessment governance, those omissions matter more than the presence of general corporate privacy language.

## Suitability for Organisational Uses

| Use case | Suitability | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Broad external benchmarking | Suitable as supplementary evidence | The test is standardised and gives broad CEFR-style output. |
| Broad initial placement support | Supplementary only | It may help with coarse grouping, but should not replace local evidence. |
| Fine-grained placement | Not recommended as primary evidence | Subscore reliability, measurement error, form equivalence, and aggregation rules are unavailable. |
| Short-cycle pre/post progress measurement | Not recommended as primary evidence | The 0701/0706 comparison shows large short-interval movement without evidence explaining meaningful change or normal variation. |
| Course design | Limited usefulness | The reports do not provide enough performance-specific diagnostic detail. |
| Teacher assignment | Limited usefulness | The output is not granular enough to guide teacher matching by specific learner needs. |
| Individual diagnostic assessment | Limited usefulness | Feedback appears category-by-level based and is not anchored to candidate utterances. |

### Specific implication for reliability assessment

The 0706 administration should be used in the project report as evidence that repeated PROGOS administrations can produce materially different profiles. It should not be described as a formal test-retest reliability result because:

- the forms differed
- the Part 5 topic differed
- the Part 3 and Part 4 prompts differed
- Dave intervened during the 0706 Part 5 preparation period
- no official measurement-error or form-equivalence information is available

The correct conclusion is not `PROGOS is unreliable`. The correct conclusion is:

> Reliability and score-change interpretation are not established from the available evidence, and the observed two-administration comparison shows enough short-interval movement and administration sensitivity to make primary use for fine placement or short-cycle progress measurement risky.

## Limitations of This Review

This report has clear limits:

- Only two full observed administrations for the same candidate were available.
- The two Aozawa administrations were not controlled parallel-form reliability administrations.
- The second administration included an external intervention during Part 5 preparation.
- Only three score reports were available for report-template comparison.
- No internal technical or psychometric documents were available.
- No vendor clarification process was conducted from within this evidence set.
- Some earlier supporting markdown files display Japanese as mojibake in PowerShell; where possible, this report relies on verified translations, direct PDF extraction, transcript evidence, and workbook references.
- The report evaluates the available artifacts; it does not establish how PROGOS performs across a large candidate population.

## Conclusions and Recommendations

PROGOS is strongest as a practical, standardised, automated speaking assessment for broad external benchmarking. The observed task mix gives real evidence of spoken production, short prompted response, discourse organisation, graph description, and pronunciation-related performance.

Its main weakness is interpretive. The observed test evidence is stronger for production than for live interaction, yet Interaction is reported as a scored category. The reports are readable, but their comments and study advice appear largely level-based and are not tied to specific response evidence. The first Aozawa report did not identify a major Part 5 role misunderstanding. The second Aozawa administration improved the available evidence base but also showed that score profiles can change substantially over a short interval under non-equivalent form and administration conditions.

The public legal pages clarify data handling and operational policies, but they do not fill the core assessment-evidence gap. The repo still lacks the psychometric, reliability, scoring-method, form-equivalence, measurement-error, and governance documentation needed to support stronger uses.

For progress measurement, the problem should be stated precisely. PROGOS may detect movement between its reported bands, including finer labels such as `A2 High` and `B1 High`, but the public materials do not explain how much movement is meaningful, how stable those classifications are, or how much normal variation should be expected. The Aozawa 0701/0706 comparison makes that limitation concrete.

### Recommendations

1. Treat PROGOS as supplementary external evidence, not as a replacement for the organisation's existing assessment system.
2. Do not use the current evidence base alone to justify primary use for fine placement, short-cycle progress measurement, course design, teacher assignment, or individual diagnostic planning.
3. If broader use is being considered, request from PROGOS:
   - validation evidence
   - reliability evidence
   - form-equivalence evidence
   - measurement-error or minimum-detectable-change guidance
   - subscore interpretation guidance
   - score-review and human-review procedures
   - task-to-score mapping information
   - explanation of how analytic scores and overall levels are derived
4. If local adoption is still of interest, run a small internal validation study comparing PROGOS against the organisation's current assessment on shared candidates and shared decisions.
5. For any local repeat-testing pilot, control the administration more tightly than the 0701/0706 evidence set:
   - no external clarification during the test
   - document whether forms are identical or parallel
   - record time between administrations
   - compare PROGOS score changes with independent human ratings
   - document whether observed changes are large enough to affect actual placement decisions
