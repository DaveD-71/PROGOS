# PROGOS Prior Analysis Retention

Last audited: `2026-07-03T16:05:24.0634188+09:00`

This file retains the final evaluative judgments established from the project evidence. Where earlier discussion was corrected, the corrected judgment below supersedes the earlier one.

Direct test, report, workbook, transcript, and legal artifacts are the evidence base. Earlier reports and ChatGPT turns record the provenance of the analysis but are not independent evidence. ## 1. Assessment instrument

### Overall construct

- The observed test samples spoken production more directly and comprehensively than live interactional competence. Presentations, short prompted answers, reading aloud, and graph description all produce useful speech samples, but the test contains no genuinely co-constructed conversation. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-114; CHAT-142]`
- The six analytic categories do not have equally direct task support. Range, Fluency, Coherence, Accuracy, and Phonology can be observed across production tasks to differing degrees; Interaction has the weakest direct evidence because the purported conversation uses fixed prerecorded prompts. Presenting all six ratings with equal apparent precision obscures this difference in evidential support. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-114]`

### Task-level judgments

- **Part 1 — Interview:** The ten questions sample spontaneous short responses across familiar and workplace topics. Despite the name, this is prompted individual production rather than an interviewer–candidate exchange. Item 7, “How do you imagine your company this time next year?”, is understandable but under-specified and non-idiomatic for the intended request. `Imagine` normally takes a direct object, a clause, a wh-clause, or an object plus a complement; here the listener must supply the missing idea of what the company *will be like*. A natural version is “What do you imagine your company will be like this time next year?” This unnecessary processing burden can affect the response independently of speaking ability. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; CAMBRIDGE-IMAGINE]`
- **Part 2 — Reading aloud:** This task provides controlled evidence of decoding, pronunciation, stress, rhythm, and limited sentence-level fluency, but little evidence of spontaneous communication, Range, Coherence, or Interaction. Its workplace authenticity is low. Item 5, “Until when can we request for changes to our urgent orders?”, contains a verb-complement error: standard English uses transitive `request` with a direct object (`request changes`), while `request for changes` requires `request` to be a noun. `By when can we request changes to our urgent orders?` is the natural deadline question. Other confirmed item-quality concerns are the awkward `shift our production to somewhere in Southeast Asia`, the unusual term `Information Tech company`, and the contextually odd `their existing products ... on Thursday`. These formulations can produce hesitation, repair, or altered prosody for reasons unrelated to the pronunciation and fluency being assessed. The items also span markedly different lexical demands, but the available evidence does not establish whether this reflects deliberate calibration; the defensible finding is heterogeneous difficulty, not an uncalibrated item bank. `[ITEMS; CHAT-106; CAMBRIDGE-REQUEST]`
- **Part 3 — Presentation:** This is a strong sustained-production task for Range, Accuracy, Fluency, and Coherence. The colleague prompt is workplace-relevant, but it favours candidates with employment experience and could be made more inclusive by allowing study experience. `[ITEMS; CHAT-106]`
- **Part 4 — Graphics presentation:** This is one of the strongest business-relevant tasks because it elicits description, comparison, numerical language, selection of information, and discourse organisation. It also measures graph literacy and time-constrained interpretation, so performance is not attributable to speaking ability alone. The instruction `the revenue of Country A and Country B's advertising industry` is awkward; `advertising-industry revenue in Countries A and B` is clearer. The fictional countries reduce prior cultural-knowledge demands in this item, but one item cannot establish low cultural bias for the test as a whole. `[ITEMS; CHAT-110; V4-ISSUES]`
- **Part 5 — Communication activity:** The topic and communicative functions are realistic, but the format measures responses to a sequence of fixed prompts more than Interaction in the CEFR sense. The candidate cannot negotiate meaning, request clarification and receive a contingent response, repair a shared misunderstanding, manage turns, initiate a topic, or influence the direction of the exchange. This is the strongest observed construct-validity concern in the test. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-110; CHAT-114; CHAT-142]`
- Aozawa persistently misunderstood the Part 5 scenario and his role within it. The intended role was to advise a speaker who was preparing to apply for a job. Instead, Aozawa repeatedly answered as an employer or recruiter: he described `my company` and its departments instead of identifying documents to bring; described workplace dress rules and his own T-shirt instead of interview clothing; explained salary and holiday conditions instead of whether those subjects should be raised during the interview; and ended by inviting the speaker to apply and contact him. This was a task-level misunderstanding across all four prompts, not merely one irrelevant response. The resulting speech cannot be interpreted straightforwardly as performance on the intended communication task. `[ITEMS; AUDIO]`

### Instructions, fairness, and accessibility

- The visible instructions for Parts 2–5 are concise and operationally clear. The listening demand is not limited to instructions: all Part 1 questions and the conversational prompts in Part 5 are delivered by audio, and Part 1 questions are not displayed. The test therefore samples comprehension of spoken prompts as a prerequisite to producing an answer. If the intended interpretation is speaking ability alone, this creates a plausible source of construct-irrelevant difficulty for lower-level candidates. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-9]`
- Instruction language must be selected according to the intended construct. L2 instructions are defensible if the test intends to measure functioning in English-only situations; L1 instructions can improve validity when the intended construct is spoken production because they prevent task instructions from becoming an additional comprehension test. PROGOS provided no L1 support for the Part 5 task setup or role. Aozawa's persistent role misunderstanding is direct evidence that the predicted risk materialised in this administration. The transcript alone cannot prove that L2-only instructions were the sole cause, but task-instruction comprehension clearly confounded the intended Part 5 performance. `[AUDIO; ITEMS; CHAT-9]`
- Several prompts assume workplace experience or familiarity with professional communication. That is relevant if the intended domain is business English, but it may disadvantage students, younger candidates, or people without office experience. `[ITEMS; CHAT-106]`
- The available evidence does not support conclusions about disability accommodations, accent or speech-recognition bias, microphone effects, or digital access. These remain unevaluated rather than satisfactory. The legal terms contain some technical-interruption and re-examination rules, but they do not establish the incidence of failures, the quality of support, or the fairness of outcomes. `[V4-ISSUES; LEGAL-TERMS]`

## 2. Reliability, scoring, and interpretation

- Fixed prompts, timing, automated delivery, and a standard report format support consistent administration. They do **not** establish score reliability. `[REPORT-V1; V4-ISSUES; CHAT-272]`
- Overall score reliability cannot be determined from the reviewed materials. No evidence was available on test–retest stability, form equivalence, measurement error, classification consistency, subscore reliability, or agreement between automated scores and qualified human ratings. The correct conclusion is “not established from the available evidence,” not “PROGOS is unreliable.” `[LEGAL-MAP; REPORT-V1; V4-ISSUES]`
- The second Aozawa administration on 2026-07-06 adds useful repeat-administration evidence but is not a controlled test-retest reliability study. The two administrations occurred five days apart, used different forms and topics, and the second administration included an external intervention by Dave during Part 5 preparation. The resulting comparison can show comparability and interpretation risks, but cannot isolate score reliability. `[AOZ2; ITEMS2; AUDIO2; REPORT-FINAL]`
- Aozawa's score profile changed substantially between 2026-07-01 and 2026-07-06: Overall `A2 High` to `B1 High`; Range `B1` to `B2 and above`; Accuracy `A2` to `B1`; Fluency `B2 and above` to `B1`; Interaction `A2` to `B1`; Coherence `B1` to `B2 and above`; Phonology unchanged at `B1`. This does not prove unreliability, but it makes score-change interpretation and short-cycle pre/post use risky without measurement-error, form-equivalence, and minimum-meaningful-change guidance. `[AOZ; AOZ2; REPORT-FINAL]`
- The 2026-07-06 Part 5 intervention is direct evidence that administration conditions can affect the task-comprehension problem identified in 2026-07-01. In 0701, Aozawa misunderstood the Communication Activity role across all four prompts. In 0706, Dave clarified the interpretation of `training attended` during preparation, and Aozawa's responses were more aligned with the intended employee-training scenario. This supports the judgment that Part 5 performance can be materially affected by task/role comprehension and administration conditions. `[AUDIO; AUDIO2; ITEMS; ITEMS2]`
- With two observed Aozawa administrations, item or form reuse can be evaluated only narrowly for these two forms. The observed forms were not identical: Part 1 questions, Part 2 sentences, Part 3 topic, Part 4 graph, and Part 5 scenario differed. This reduces direct test-retest comparability and means the project still lacks official form-equivalence evidence. `[ITEMS; ITEMS2; AUDIO; AUDIO2]`
- The reviewed materials do not explain how tasks contribute to the six analytic ratings, how those ratings are aggregated, or how the overall level is derived. This limits interpretation of both the overall result and differences among subscores. `[AOZ; SAKI; LEGAL-MAP]`
- Do not frame the evaluation as verification or rebuttal of supposed PROGOS “claims.” The earlier discussion incorrectly attributed a series of claims to PROGOS—not only reliability or validity claims, but also proposed commercial claims about what the test measures, CEFR alignment, automated scoring, and identification of detailed abilities. The user explicitly rejected that entire claims-based line of argument as unsupported and irrelevant. The evaluation must instead state only what is directly observable in the test, reports, and captured documents, with an exact source for every attributed PROGOS statement. `[CHAT-233; CHAT-246; CHAT-247; CHAT-249]`

## 3. Score-report evaluation

### Overall Assessment

- The Overall Assessment is not a useful or accurate synthesis of overall speaking performance. Both sentences focus primarily on lexical resources: simple words, phrases, and sentences, followed by a “broad range of basic vocabulary and expressions.” Interaction is mentioned only as a context in which those lexical resources are used. `[AOZ; SAKI; CHAT-130; CHAT-142]`
- Read in isolation, the wording is clear, encouraging, and broadly compatible with an upper-A2 description. The problem is its scope and interpretation, not basic readability. `A broad range of basic vocabulary` is semantically awkward and potentially inflates the impression of A2 language range. `[AOZ; CHAT-123]`
- The summary omits substantive information about Accuracy, Fluency, Coherence, and Phonology and says almost nothing about the quality of Interaction. Calling it an “Overall Assessment” invites a multidimensional interpretation that its wording does not support. `[CHAT-130; CHAT-142]`
- The summary generalises to interaction in “common everyday situations,” while the observed task evidence is weighted toward workplace topics, monologic production, and fixed prompted exchanges. It therefore supports a narrower inference than the real-world statement implies. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; AOZ; CHAT-123]`
- The report uses unqualified `You can` statements rather than tying the interpretation to the observed administration. This presents an inference from a limited task sample as a general fact about the candidate and hides normal uncertainty in score interpretation. `[AOZ; CHAT-123; CHAT-142]`
- The Aozawa and Saki reports use the same Overall Assessment and both report `A2 High`, despite Aozawa receiving `B2 and above` for Fluency and Saki receiving B1. This reinforces the judgment that the summary is a level-based lexical template rather than a synthesis of each candidate’s analytic profile. `[AOZ; SAKI]`

- The Range wording makes B1 sound materially stronger than the official descriptor by replacing hesitation, circumlocution, repetition, and formulation difficulty with a statement of clear, direct, contextually appropriate expression across a wide range of topics. `[AOZ; CHAT-142]`
- The Interaction wording has two separate problems: it does not match the selected official A2 descriptor as closely as the A2+ descriptor, and the test design does not give the candidate a real opportunity to lead or follow a jointly managed conversation. `[AOZ; ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-114]`
- The completed workbook comparison for Aozawa 0706 established that Fluency, Interaction, and Phonology are acceptable same-competence matches, while Range, Accuracy, and Coherence align more closely with neighbouring workbook descriptors than with the reported/proxy level. Range `B2 and above` is closer to B2 General linguistic range than the B2+ proxy; Accuracy B1 wording is closer to B1+ because it explicitly mentions first-language influence; Coherence `B2 and above` is closer to B2 because it explicitly says a limited number of linking expressions. `[AOZ2; CEFR]`

### Diagnostic usefulness and individualisation

- The reports are clear, readable, and potentially motivating, but they are weak as individual diagnostic reports. They do not cite anything the candidate actually said, identify task-specific successes or errors, explain why a rating was awarded, or connect advice to observed performance. `[AOZ; SAKI; CHAT-142; CHAT-171]`
- Comparison of the two reports provides direct evidence of category-by-level templating. Aozawa and Saki have the same overall level and the same ratings for Range, Accuracy, Interaction, Coherence, and Phonology; those sections contain substantively the same comments, advice, examples, and references. Fluency is the one differing rating, and its associated text changes. The defensible conclusion is that report content is selected primarily by category and reported level, not generated as an individual analysis of the response evidence. `[AOZ; SAKI]`
- Generic advice is not inherently poor pedagogy, but the report presents it as feedback on the individual assessment. Examples such as practising `I wish`, using `Hmm` for disagreement, opening opinions with `In my opinion`, and watching TED Talks are not shown to follow from anything observed in the candidate’s performance. `[AOZ; SAKI; CHAT-114; CHAT-142]`
- Some advice changes construct: `Hmm` is pragmatic/discourse advice rather than evidence-based Fluency feedback; opinion-opening formulas do not develop conversational management; general English exposure and TED Talks are not a targeted diagnosis of the candidate’s phonology. `[AOZ; CHAT-142]`
- The report also generalises to broad work, leisure, social, and formality contexts that were sampled only narrowly or not at all in the observed administration. These comments are better treated as level-based guidance than as demonstrated context-specific ability. `[AOZ; ITEMS; AUDIO; CHAT-114]`
- The Aozawa report does not identify the persistent Part 5 role misunderstanding or explain its effect on Task Achievement or Interaction. Instead, it supplies the same generic Interaction descriptor and study guidance found in Saki's same-level report. This is direct evidence that the learner-facing feedback is not anchored to a major observed feature of Aozawa's actual performance. `[AUDIO; AOZ; SAKI]`
- Templated feedback does not by itself prove that the underlying scores are invalid. It shows that the learner-facing explanations and study advice have limited individual diagnostic value. The assessment instrument, scoring, and report content must remain separate objects of evaluation. `[CHAT-171; CHAT-272]`

### Score-profile transparency

- The relation between analytic ratings and `A2 High` is unexplained. Saki has four B1 ratings and two A2 ratings; Aozawa has three B1 ratings, one `B2 and above` rating, and two A2 ratings. Both receive the same overall result and summary. The available evidence does not justify inferring the weighting or aggregation rule. `[AOZ; SAKI]`
- Labels such as `A2 High`, plus levels, and `B2 and above` show that PROGOS does not report only the six main CEFR bands. These finer labels do not resolve the interpretive problem because the report does not define their cut scores, stability, measurement error, or minimum meaningful change. `[AOZ; SAKI; CHAT-225; CHAT-228]`

## 4. Organisational suitability

- The observed design supports administrative convenience: the test is short, standardised, delivered without a live examiner, and produces a consistent report format. That does not establish total practicality. Cost, technical requirements, support arrangements, procurement burden, failure rates, implementation risk, and the operational consequences of retesting were not evaluated comprehensively. `[ITEMS; AUDIO; LEGAL-TERMS; V4-ISSUES]`
- It may have supplementary value as a broad external benchmark or as one input to broad initial placement. It should not be the sole or primary basis for consequential placement decisions. `[REPORT-V1; REPORT-FINAL]`
- It is not recommended as the primary tool for fine-grained placement, short-cycle pre/post measurement, course design, teacher assignment, or individual diagnostic planning. These uses require more sensitive change information and more performance-specific diagnosis than the reviewed reports provide. `[REPORT-V1; REPORT-FINAL; CHAT-228; CHAT-272]`
- For pre/post use, the important issue is not simply that CEFR has six main levels. CEFR proficiency bands are broad, while typical corporate courses may produce worthwhile gains that do not cross a reported band. PROGOS uses finer labels, but the reviewed materials provide no minimum detectable change, measurement-error information, or explanation of how to interpret movement among those labels. An unchanged result could therefore conceal useful learning, while a changed result cannot be interpreted confidently as real change from the available evidence. `[CHAT-225; CHAT-228; REPORT-FINAL]`
- Consequentially, stakeholders could interpret an unchanged reported level as no improvement, treat generic advice as an individual diagnosis, or make course and teacher decisions from evidence that is insufficiently specific for those purposes. These are risks of the proposed uses, not proof that such consequences have already occurred. `[REPORT-V1; CHAT-272]`
- The organisation’s internal 1.0–9.0 scale with 0.25 increments is more granular for operational ranking, but finer numerical increments alone do not prove greater measurement sensitivity or validity. A rigorous comparison must also consider construct coverage, rating consistency, measurement error, and decision thresholds for the internal test. `[V4-ISSUES]`

## 5. Legal and governance evaluation

- The current public pages are materially more informative than the older corporate privacy-management page. They identify voice data, monitoring-camera images, employer or service-provider disclosure in applicable contracts, outsourcing and re-outsourcing, international handling including Japan and the Philippines, and data-subject rights. `[LEGAL-MAP; LEGAL-PRIVACY; LEGAL-TERMS]`
- The privacy page states that personal data are retained indefinitely unless deletion is requested and then deleted within one month. This gives a deletion mechanism but leaves voice and monitoring data subject to open-ended default retention. `[LEGAL-PRIVACY]`
- The terms allow anonymised statistical data derived from contract-related information to be used for future product or service development and sales activity. This is a documented secondary-use basis and should be disclosed in any governance summary rather than inferred as unspecified AI training. `[LEGAL-KIYAKU; LEGAL-MAP]`
- The terms address some failures: an interrupted unmonitored test may resume from the interruption point, a monitored test restarts from the beginning, and re-examination following communication problems or applicant-attributable recording defects may require a new application and fee. These rules clarify contractual allocation of responsibility but do not show how often failures occur or whether the resulting treatment is fair in practice. `[LEGAL-TERMS]`
- The test-taker terms prohibit recording test content, taking screenshots, and disclosing questions, and they reserve intellectual-property rights in PROGOS content and results. This is relevant to how captured test evidence may be stored, circulated, or published; the assessment review itself does not determine the legal status of any particular use. `[LEGAL-TERMS; LEGAL-KIYAKU]`
- The terms state that scoring uses proprietary criteria, inquiries about results or test content will not be answered, and no representation or warranty is made regarding the completeness or accuracy of results. The reviewed pages provide no explicit score-appeal or human-review procedure. This is a significant assessment-governance limitation where results may inform organisational decisions. `[LEGAL-MAP; LEGAL-TERMS]`
- The terms permit PROGOS content and the terms themselves to be changed with notice. No evidence shows that such changes have affected these administrations, but longitudinal or form-comparability work would need to record the version and date of the test and governing documents rather than assume stability. `[LEGAL-TERMS]`
- The legal pages do not supply psychometric validation, a scoring manual, reliability evidence, task-to-score mappings, or AI-model governance details. Their data-handling disclosures must not be treated as evidence of assessment quality. `[LEGAL-MAP]`

## 6. Corrections that govern future analysis

- Do not describe the Overall Assessment as mainly an Interaction descriptor. The retained judgment is that it is overwhelmingly about vocabulary and basic expression and only touches on Interaction. `[CHAT-127; CHAT-130]`
- Do not treat standardisation or automated delivery as proof of score reliability. `[V4-ISSUES; CHAT-272]`
- Do not invent, infer, catalogue, verify, or rebut PROGOS “claims” of any kind. If a PROGOS position must be attributed, quote the exact reviewed source and keep the attribution limited to what that source actually says. Otherwise, evaluate only the observed test, reports, and documents. `[CHAT-233; CHAT-246; CHAT-247; CHAT-249]`
- Do not infer that templated report text invalidates the underlying scores. It directly limits report individualisation and diagnostic usefulness; score quality is a separate question. `[CHAT-171; CHAT-272]`
- Do not argue that PROGOS has only six coarse CEFR levels, and do not assume that plus/high labels solve progress sensitivity. The unresolved issue is the absence of evidence explaining score stability and meaningful change. `[CHAT-225; CHAT-228]`
