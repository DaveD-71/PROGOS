# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test

Prepared: 2026-07-03

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates PROGOS as:

- a speaking test
- a score-reporting system
- a possible tool for organisational decision-making

The main conclusion is cautious. PROGOS appears usable as a broad, standardised external speaking benchmark, but the current evidence base does not justify using it as a primary tool for fine-grained placement, short-cycle progress measurement, course design, teacher assignment, or individual diagnostic feedback.

That conclusion is driven by four observed features.

First, the test samples several useful spoken-production abilities through multiple task types. Second, the strongest direct evidence concerns monologic production and short prompted responses, not live co-constructed interaction. Third, the score reports are clear and fast to read, but much of the feedback appears level-based and templated rather than tightly performance-specific. Fourth, the public legal pages clarify some operational and data-handling points, but they do not provide the psychometric, scoring-method, reliability, or governance evidence needed for stronger claims.

## Test Purpose and Observed Design

### Observed purpose

From the score reports and public terms, PROGOS is presented as a business English speaking test that reports a CEFR-aligned overall level and six analytic category ratings. The user-policy page states that PROGOS uses automated voice delivery and that results are scored according to the company’s own criteria.

### Observed test structure

The Aozawa administration provides the clearest direct task evidence.

Observed parts:

1. Interview
2. Reading aloud
3. Presentation
4. Graphics presentation
5. Communication activity

Observed timing and prompt conditions from the screenshot set and test-only transcript:

- Part 1: 10 interview questions, 20 seconds speaking time per question
- Part 2: 8 read-aloud sentences, 10 seconds per sentence
- Part 3: 40 seconds preparation, 60 seconds speaking
- Part 4: 40 seconds preparation, 60 seconds speaking
- Part 5: 40 seconds preparation, then 30 seconds speaking time after each audio prompt

Observed Part 5 evidence from the cleaned transcript shows a sequence of fixed audio prompts rather than live branching interaction.

### What the task mix samples well

The observed form gives direct evidence of:

- short-response spontaneous speaking
- sustained individual speaking
- basic discourse organisation
- some pronunciation and prosodic control
- response under time pressure

### What the task mix samples less directly

The observed form gives weaker direct evidence of:

- negotiation of meaning
- repair after misunderstanding in a genuinely interactive exchange
- turn-taking management
- responsiveness to unpredictable interlocutor moves
- co-construction of meaning over multiple turns

That matters because one reported analytic category is Interaction.

## Task and Construct Analysis

### Part 1: Interview

This part elicits short responses to familiar and workplace-related prompts. It is useful for spontaneous language sampling, but it is still a prompted monologic format rather than a true interviewer-candidate exchange.

### Part 2: Reading aloud

This part gives direct evidence of decoding, pronunciation, stress, and fluency under controlled conditions. It also adds administrative standardisation. Its weakness is construct fit: it is less communicative than the other parts and partly reflects reading accuracy rather than spoken interaction.

### Part 3: Presentation

This part gives useful evidence of sustained production, coherence, and lexical range. It is more relevant to professional speaking than read-aloud tasks. Its limitation is that success may also depend on topic familiarity and the ability to maintain a one-minute monologue under preparation pressure.

### Part 4: Graphics presentation

This part is one of the stronger business-relevant tasks. It elicits summarising, comparing, and interpreting visual information. However, it also introduces construct-irrelevant demands tied to graph interpretation and time-constrained planning.

### Part 5: Communication activity

This is the most important validity issue in the observed test design. The task is presented as a conversation, but the captured evidence shows fixed prerecorded prompts with fixed response windows. That supports prompted response ability, but not full live interaction. A candidate cannot ask for clarification in a natural way, negotiate meaning, respond to emergent turns, or shape the conversation jointly with a real interlocutor.

The result is that the observed test supports spoken production more strongly than it supports interactional competence.

## CEFR Alignment and Score-Report Analysis

### Important scope point

The overall assessment summary in the report is not itself a CEFR competence in the descriptor workbook. It should not be treated as a direct CEFR descriptor match.

### Reported analytic categories

The six report categories are:

- Range
- Accuracy
- Fluency
- Interaction
- Coherence
- Phonology

### Aozawa report: validated CEFR comparison

Using `CEFR Descriptors_en.xlsx` and the validated comparison in `test reports/Aozawa/0701/report/PROGOS Aozawa's result_260701_evaluation_comments_bilingual.md`, the findings are:

| Category | Reported level | Same-competence same-level CEFR match? |
|---|---:|---|
| Range | B1 | No |
| Accuracy | A2 | Yes |
| Fluency | B2 and above | Yes |
| Interaction | A2 | No |
| Coherence | B1 | Yes |
| Phonology | B1 | Yes |

The two mismatches are the most important.

### Range

The PROGOS Range comment is broader and more assertive than the official B1 descriptor for General linguistic range. The validated closest wording match points upward to a higher-level descriptor and away from the official B1 same-competence wording. That suggests the report comment may overstate what the cited CEFR level would usually imply.

### Interaction

The PROGOS Interaction comment says the candidate can exchange information on familiar everyday topics when the other person leads the conversation. That wording is closer to a supported structured-interaction descriptor than to the exact official same-level descriptor used in the workbook comparison. More importantly, the underlying observed task evidence is not strong live-interaction evidence in the first place.

### Meaning of `A2 High` and `B2 and above`

The reports do not use only the six plain CEFR labels. The overall level includes a finer label, `A2 High`, which indicates a sub-band style interpretation rather than a simple six-band report. At the same time, Aozawa’s Fluency rating is reported as `B2 and above`, which is not a standard closed CEFR band label in the way A2, B1, or B2 are.

Taken together, these labels suggest a reporting system that mixes CEFR-style levels with proprietary finer or open-ended banding. The practical issue is not simply that the scale is “too broad.” The issue is that the public report does not explain how much score movement is needed before a change should be interpreted as real progress rather than normal measurement variation.

### Evidence of templating across reports

The two score reports provide strong evidence that much of the feedback is templated by level and category.

Observed cross-report pattern:

- Aozawa and Saki share the same overall level: `A2 High`
- both reports share the same ratings for Range, Accuracy, Interaction, Coherence, and Phonology
- for those shared categories, the report comments, study advice, and example content are substantively the same
- the one clear difference is Fluency, where Saki is reported at B1 and Aozawa at `B2 and above`, and the Fluency comment/advice changes accordingly

This supports a narrow claim: the reporting system appears to use category-by-level text blocks rather than feedback closely tied to the specific performance evidence in an individual administration.

That is not inherently invalid. It does, however, reduce the report’s diagnostic value.

## Reliability and Interpretation Limits

The observed design supports administrative consistency:

- fixed prompts
- fixed timings
- automated audio delivery
- standard report format

Those features support standardisation, but they do not establish score reliability.

The repo does not contain evidence about:

- test-retest stability
- inter-form equivalence
- measurement error
- classification consistency
- subscore reliability
- scoring consistency against human ratings

The legal pages instead explicitly state that PROGOS uses the company’s own criteria and that the company does not make representations or warranties about the completeness or accuracy of results. That is operationally important. It does not prove unreliability, but it does reinforce that reliability cannot be assumed from the available materials.

## Fairness, Accessibility, and Practicality

### Fairness and accessibility

Observed strengths:

- standardised delivery
- mostly familiar topic domains
- short administration windows that are operationally simple

Observed concerns:

- several prompts assume comfort with workplace discourse
- the graph task adds visual-interpretive demands
- time pressure may amplify planning-speed differences that are not purely speaking ability
- the observed interaction evidence depends on responding to fixed audio prompts rather than managing a live exchange

The available evidence does not allow a strong judgment on disability accommodations, speech-recognition bias, accent bias, or technical-failure handling beyond what is generally described in the terms.

### Practicality

Practicality is one of PROGOS’s clearest strengths in the available evidence. The test is short, standardised, and reportable through an existing platform. The user-policy page also states that results are posted on the examinee site within five business days after the test.

That operational practicality should still be separated from evidential claims about interpretation quality.

## Legal and Data-Governance Observations

This section is descriptive, not legal advice.

### What the public pages clearly state

From the captured `kiyaku` and `user_policy` pages:

- PROGOS uses automated voice delivery
- scores are based on the company’s own evaluation criteria
- results are posted within five business days
- the company may outsource part or all of PROGOS and related procedures, including re-outsourcing
- the company does not respond to inquiries about test results or test content
- the company disclaims representations or warranties regarding the completeness or accuracy of results

From the captured `user_policy` and `privacy` pages:

- audio is recorded during the test
- for online monitoring, PC-camera image data may also be collected
- collected personal data include name, email address, voice data, IP address, cookie information, and website access history
- stated purposes include evaluating the service and analysing test results
- data may be handled across multiple countries; Japan and the Philippines are named
- personal data are retained unless deletion is requested; after a deletion request, deletion is stated to occur within one month
- the pages describe data-subject rights, including access, correction, deletion, restriction, objection, portability, and consent withdrawal

The older `privacy-management` page is broader and less test-specific than the newer `progos.ai` privacy pages.

### What remains unclear or unavailable

The public pages reviewed do not provide:

- psychometric validation evidence
- a scoring manual
- a technical explanation of score generation
- explicit routine retention periods for voice data absent a deletion request
- explicit routine retention periods for monitoring images absent a deletion request
- a clear human review or score appeal procedure
- explicit AI-model governance detail for the scoring process

For assessment governance, those omissions matter more than the presence of general corporate privacy language.

## Suitability for Organisational Uses

Judgments in this section are based on the observed task evidence, the two score reports, the validated CEFR comparison work, and the legal-document evidence above.

| Use case | Suitability | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Broad external benchmarking | Reasonably suitable as a supplement | Standardised format and broad CEFR-style reporting support high-level benchmarking. |
| Broad initial placement support | Supplementary only | May help with coarse grouping, but report detail is not strong enough for fine placement decisions by itself. |
| Fine-grained placement | Not recommended as primary evidence | The available score evidence is too coarse and the reliability evidence is missing. |
| Short-cycle pre/post progress measurement | Not recommended as primary evidence | The reports use finer labels such as `A2 High`, but the public materials do not explain the amount of movement needed to count as real progress or provide measurement-error evidence. |
| Course design | Limited usefulness | The reports do not provide enough performance-specific diagnostic detail. |
| Teacher assignment | Limited usefulness | The test does not produce sufficiently granular individual diagnostic evidence for this use. |
| Individual diagnostic assessment | Limited usefulness | The feedback appears largely templated by category and level. |

## Limitations of This Review

This report has clear limits.

- Only one full observed administration was available at task level.
- Only two score reports were available for cross-report comparison.
- The validated CEFR matching work is detailed for Aozawa, not for a large report sample.
- No internal technical or psychometric documents were available.
- No vendor clarification process was conducted from within this repo evidence set.
- Some extracted Japanese text in supporting markdown files is encoding-damaged, so the report relies where possible on the verified English and on direct PDF/transcript evidence.

These limitations mean that the report should be read as an evidence-based review of the accessible materials, not as a final validation study.

## Conclusions and Recommendations

PROGOS looks strongest as a practical, standardised, automated speaking assessment for broad external benchmarking. The observed task mix gives real evidence of spoken production, short prompted response, discourse organisation, and pronunciation-related performance.

Its main weakness is interpretive. The observed test evidence is much stronger for production than for live interaction, yet Interaction is reported as a scored category. The score reports are readable, but the cross-report comparison and the category-level wording patterns suggest that much of the feedback is templated by level rather than tightly anchored to individual performance evidence.

The public legal pages clarify data handling and operational policies, but they do not fill the core evidential gap. The repo still lacks the psychometric, reliability, scoring-method, and governance documentation needed to support stronger uses.

For progress measurement in particular, the problem should be stated precisely. PROGOS may detect movement between its reported bands, including finer labels such as `A2 High`, but the public materials do not make clear how much movement is meaningful, how stable those classifications are, or how much normal variation should be expected.

### Recommended next steps

1. Treat PROGOS as supplementary external evidence, not a replacement for the organisation’s existing assessment system.
2. If broader use is being considered, request from PROGOS:
   - validation evidence
   - subscore interpretation guidance
   - reliability evidence
   - score-review and human-review procedures
   - technical information on how analytic scores and overall levels are derived
3. If local adoption is still of interest, run a small internal validation study comparing PROGOS against the organisation’s current assessment on shared candidates and shared decisions.
4. Do not use the current public evidence base alone to justify primary use for fine placement, short-cycle progress measurement, or individual diagnostic planning.
