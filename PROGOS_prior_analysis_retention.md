# PROGOS Prior Analysis Retention

Saved on: `2026-07-03T15:12:00+09:00`

This file keeps only retained evaluative judgments that should not need to be rediscovered.

## Report-level judgments

- The PROGOS score report is clearer as a summary output than as a diagnostic instrument.
- The report feedback has limited value for course design, teacher assignment, or detailed individual learning planning.
- The report comments and advice appear more level-based than performance-specific.
- Any strong claim that the reports are templated should still be tied to explicit side-by-side evidence, but the retained working judgment is that the feedback is not highly individualised.

## CEFR-related judgments

- The Overall Assessment summary is not a good summary of overall speaking performance.
- In the Aozawa report, the two Overall Assessment comments both describe vocabulary / language range and basic expression in everyday situations, rather than overall performance across the reported competences.
- Therefore, the Overall Assessment wording is not especially beneficial or accurate as an overall performance summary.
- CEFR matching should be limited to the six named analytic competences.
- Retained Aozawa CEFR judgment:
  - Range: mismatch
  - Accuracy: acceptable same-competence same-level match
  - Fluency: acceptable same-competence same-level match
  - Interaction: mismatch
  - Coherence: acceptable same-competence same-level match
  - Phonology: acceptable same-competence same-level match
- The Range comment is broader and more assertive than the official B1 descriptor for the same competence.
- The Interaction comment is not a good same-competence same-level CEFR match.

## Test-design judgments

- The observed PROGOS task set supports judgments about spoken production more strongly than judgments about live interactional competence.
- The available evidence supports analysis of prompted speaking, reading aloud, presentation, and business-response tasks.
- The available evidence does not justify treating the test as strong direct evidence of live co-constructed interaction with a human interlocutor.

## Score-interpretation judgments

- PROGOS uses finer reporting labels than the six coarse CEFR bands, including labels such as `A2 High / A2+`, `B1+`, and `B2+`.
- Therefore, the strongest criticism is not simply that CEFR bands are too broad.
- The stronger retained judgment is that the public evidence does not show how much score movement is meaningful, how stable the scores are, or how results should be interpreted for fine-grained progress decisions.

## Organisational-use judgments

- PROGOS appears usable as a broad, standardised external speaking benchmark.
- The current evidence base does not justify using PROGOS as a primary tool for:
  - fine-grained placement
  - short-cycle progress measurement
  - course design
  - teacher assignment
  - detailed diagnostic feedback

## Legal / governance judgments

- The public legal and policy pages are useful for documenting what PROGOS states about operation, terms, and some data-handling matters.
- Those pages do not provide sufficient public evidence for strong claims about:
  - scoring methodology
  - psychometric quality
  - reliability
  - item-bank design
  - AI scoring governance

## Judgments about the earlier draft report

- `PROGOS_test_evaluation_report_v4.md` was not a defensible evaluation report.
- It was better understood as an outline-level position paper with conclusions stated more strongly than the documented evidence allowed.
- Its main weaknesses were lack of traceable evidence, lack of explicit method, shallow treatment of validity/reliability, and failure to incorporate the completed CEFR analysis.
