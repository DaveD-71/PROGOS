# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates the PROGOS English speaking test on the basis of materials reviewed during this project, including publicly accessible PROGOS information, screenshots of test items, candidate score reports, and the PROGOS terms and privacy-management pages.

The evaluation distinguishes between:

1. the assessment instrument itself;
2. the score reports and learning feedback generated by the system; and
3. the suitability of the test for the organisation's assessment purposes.

PROGOS is a fully automated, machine-run, non-adaptive speaking test. It records the test taker's spoken responses and evaluates the audio after the test.

The test has several practical strengths. It is standardised, scalable, convenient, and includes several workplace-relevant speaking tasks. However, this evaluation identifies significant limitations in the areas most important for diagnostic use: measurement of interactional competence, individualised feedback, transparency of score interpretation, and suitability for measuring short-term training gains.

The overall conclusion is that PROGOS may be useful as a broad indicator of speaking proficiency and as a supplementary external benchmark. However, it should not be used as a replacement for a well-designed internal speaking assessment when the purpose is class allocation, pre/post-training comparison, course design, teacher assignment, or diagnosis of individual strengths and weaknesses.

---

## Key Conclusions

- The test appears stronger as a measure of spoken production than spoken interaction.
- The score reports appear to provide level-based learning guidance rather than individual diagnostic feedback.
- CEFR-style proficiency bands are too broad to function as the principal measure of short-term learning gains in most corporate training programmes.
- The information generated by PROGOS is insufficiently sensitive and insufficiently diagnostic for the purposes for which the organisation currently uses its internal assessment system.
- PROGOS should therefore be viewed, if used at all, as a supplementary external benchmark rather than a replacement for the organisation's existing assessment practices.

---

## Reliability

The test design supports consistency in administration. Prompts, timing, and delivery are standardised, and the absence of human interlocutors removes some sources of variability.

Administrative consistency, however, should not be equated with full score reliability.

From the reviewed materials it is not possible to determine:

- how stable scores are across repeated administrations;
- how equivalent different forms are;
- how much measurement error is associated with reported levels;
- whether subscores are equally reliable; or
- how the automated scoring system handles atypical but valid responses.

Accordingly, no conclusion is reached regarding overall score reliability. The reviewed evidence allows only the narrower observation that the design of the test is likely to support consistent administration.

---

## Consequential Considerations

Because the reports provide:

- broad proficiency levels;
- largely generic feedback; and
- limited diagnostic information,

there is a risk that stakeholders may:

- underestimate genuine learning gains;
- overinterpret unchanged scores;
- assume that the reports are individual diagnostic analyses;
- make instructional decisions based on insufficient evidence.

These consequences are particularly relevant when the test is used:

- before and after training;
- for teacher assignment;
- for curriculum decisions; and
- for evaluation of programme effectiveness.

---

## Suitability for the Organisation's Assessment Purposes

The organisation already employs an internally developed speaking and listening assessment system that:

- assesses six dimensions of performance;
- uses a 1.0–9.0 scale with 0.25-point increments;
- allows fine-grained ranking of learner ability; and
- supports class allocation, progress measurement, and course design.

### Student Class Allocation

PROGOS may provide a broad indication of speaking proficiency and may have some value as a supplementary external benchmark.

However, broad proficiency labels provide less differentiation among learners than the organisation's existing system.

**Recommendation:** Retain the internal assessment as the primary placement instrument.

### Pre- and Post-Training Assessment

PROGOS is not recommended for this purpose.

The issue is not that the test is automated or that it uses CEFR-aligned reporting. The issue is that CEFR proficiency bands are intentionally broad descriptors of language ability.

Many corporate training programmes are not sufficiently long to produce movement between those bands.

Consequently:

- meaningful learning gains may occur;
- communicative performance may improve substantially;
- specific weaknesses may be addressed successfully;

while the reported level remains unchanged.

An unchanged level may therefore be interpreted incorrectly as evidence that no learning occurred.

Because the feedback is largely level-based and generic, it is also unlikely to explain what changed between pre- and post-test performances.

**Recommendation:** Do not use PROGOS as the principal instrument for pre/post-training assessment.

### Course Design and Teacher Assignment

PROGOS is not recommended for these purposes.

The reviewed reports:

- do not cite specific performance evidence;
- do not identify precise linguistic weaknesses;
- do not provide sufficiently individualised diagnosis;
- provide largely generic recommendations.

Consequently, they provide limited support for:

- targeted syllabus design;
- instructor selection;
- individual intervention planning.

**Recommendation:** Do not use PROGOS for course design or teacher assignment.

---

## Final Recommendations

| Purpose | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Broad external benchmarking | Possible supplementary use |
| Initial broad placement | Possible supplementary use |
| Fine-grained placement | Not recommended |
| Pre/post-training assessment | Not recommended |
| Course design | Not recommended |
| Teacher assignment | Not recommended |
| Diagnostic assessment | Not recommended |

---

## Final Judgement

PROGOS is a practical automated speaking test with several useful task types and clear learner-facing reports. It is strongest as a convenient broad indicator of speaking proficiency.

The principal limitation of PROGOS for this organisation is not that it is automated or CEFR-aligned. Rather, it is that the information produced by the test is insufficiently sensitive and insufficiently diagnostic for the purposes for which the organisation uses its internal assessment system.

Accordingly, PROGOS should not be adopted as a replacement for the organisation's existing speaking and listening assessment practices and should only be considered, if at all, as a supplementary external benchmark.
