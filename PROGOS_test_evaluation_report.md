# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates the PROGOS English speaking test on the basis of materials reviewed during this project, including publicly accessible PROGOS information, screenshots of test items, candidate score reports, and the PROGOS terms and privacy-management pages supplied or identified during the review.

The evaluation focuses on the test as a language assessment instrument and on the usefulness of its reports for training-related decisions. It does not make claims about unpublished technical studies, internal scoring models, or psychometric evidence that was not reviewed.

PROGOS is a fully automated, machine-run speaking test. It records the test taker's spoken responses and evaluates the audio after the test. The test is non-adaptive: prompts are fixed, the system does not respond dynamically to the test taker's performance, and there is no human interlocutor during the test.

The test has clear practical strengths. It is standardised, scalable, convenient, and includes several workplace-relevant speaking tasks. However, this evaluation identifies significant limitations in the areas most important for diagnostic use: interactional competence, individualised feedback, transparency of score interpretation, and suitability for measuring short-term training gains.

The overall conclusion is that PROGOS may be useful as a broad indicator of speaking proficiency, but it should not be used as a replacement for a well-designed internal speaking assessment when the purpose is class allocation, pre/post-training comparison, course design, teacher assignment, or diagnosis of individual strengths and weaknesses.

---

## 1. Scope of Review

The review considered the following evidence:

- screenshots of PROGOS test instructions and items for Parts 2 to 5;
- extracted content from two PROGOS candidate reports;
- the structured bilingual analysis file for one candidate report;
- PROGOS privacy-management information;
- PROGOS terms of use;
- the user's description of the organisation's existing internal speaking and listening assessment system.

The review did not include:

- access to PROGOS internal scoring algorithms;
- independent technical manuals;
- unpublished reliability studies;
- unpublished validation studies;
- raw audio recordings from the candidates;
- human re-ratings of the candidate performances.

Therefore, this report evaluates the observable test design, report content, interpretive quality, and suitability for the stated organisational uses. It avoids making claims about evidence that has not been reviewed.

---

## 2. Description of the Test

PROGOS is a fully automated speaking test. Based on the materials reviewed, the test includes the following task types:

1. Interview-style spoken responses.
2. Reading aloud.
3. Presentation.
4. Graphics presentation.
5. Communication activity with fixed audio prompts.

The report provides an overall CEFR-aligned level and six analytic categories:

- Range
- Accuracy
- Fluency
- Interaction
- Coherence
- Phonology

The test is machine-run and non-adaptive. This is central to the evaluation because it affects what kinds of speaking ability the test can and cannot directly observe.

## 3. Evaluation of Test Methodology

### 3.1 Part 1: Interview

Part 1 appears to consist mainly of audio prompts requiring spoken responses. Because the reviewed screenshots did not contain the complete Part 1 content, this section cannot be evaluated in detail.

It is likely to provide evidence of spontaneous spoken production, including range, fluency, accuracy, coherence, and phonology. However, without the exact prompts, no detailed item-level judgement is made here.

### 3.2 Part 2: Reading Aloud

Part 2 requires test takers to read sentences aloud.

#### Strengths

Reading aloud is highly standardised. Every test taker receives the same sentence text, and the response format is tightly controlled. This makes it suitable for automated analysis of pronunciation, rhythm, pausing, and some aspects of oral fluency.

#### Limitations

Reading aloud provides limited evidence of communicative speaking ability. It does not require the test taker to formulate meaning, organise ideas, interact with another speaker, or respond to communicative pressure. It is therefore mainly relevant to phonology and, to a lesser extent, delivery fluency.

Some reviewed sentences were unnatural or awkward. For example:

> Until when can we request for changes to our urgent orders?

This is not idiomatic English. More natural alternatives would include:

> By when can we request changes to our urgent orders?

or:

> Until when can we request changes to our urgent orders?

Unnatural reading-aloud items can introduce construct-irrelevant difficulty. A test taker may hesitate, self-correct, or produce unusual rhythm because the sentence itself is unnatural, not because their speaking ability is weak.

#### Judgement

Reading aloud is useful for standardised phonological evidence, but it is weak as evidence of communicative speaking ability.

### 3.3 Part 3: Presentation

Part 3 asks the test taker to speak for an extended period on a given topic.

#### Strengths

This task can elicit useful evidence of:

- fluency;
- coherence;
- range;
- accuracy;
- discourse organisation;
- ability to sustain speech.

The reviewed topic about a colleague is broadly appropriate for workplace communication and can generate meaningful spoken production.

#### Limitations

The topic may favour test takers with workplace experience and confidence in discussing professional relationships. It may be less suitable for students, younger test takers, or people with limited work histories.

#### Judgement

This is one of the stronger task types in the test.

### 3.4 Part 4: Graphics Presentation

Part 4 requires the test taker to describe and compare information in a graph.

#### Strengths

This is a strong business communication task. It can elicit:

- description of trends;
- comparison;
- sequencing;
- numerical language;
- discourse organisation;
- selection of relevant information.

It is particularly relevant for business English contexts where speakers may need to explain data or summarise visual information.

#### Limitations

The task also measures graph literacy and data interpretation. This is not necessarily inappropriate for business communication, but it means the task is not purely a measure of speaking ability.

#### Judgement

This is a strong task for business-oriented speaking assessment, provided that the interpretation of scores recognises the additional cognitive demand of graph reading.

### 3.5 Part 5: Communication Activity

Part 5 asks the test taker to respond to fixed audio prompts in a simulated communicative situation.

#### Strengths

The task has a plausible communicative purpose and can elicit advice-giving, explanation, and extended spoken responses.

#### Limitations

The activity is not genuine interaction. Because the test is fully automated and non-adaptive, the system cannot:

- ask follow-up questions based on the test taker's response;
- request clarification;
- misunderstand and require repair;
- negotiate meaning;
- respond to the test taker's ideas;
- allow the test taker to lead or redirect the conversation.

This is a significant limitation because interactional competence in the CEFR sense includes turn-taking, co-construction of meaning, repair, initiative, and responsiveness to another speaker. A fixed-prompt task can measure the ability to respond to prompts, but it cannot fully measure interaction.

#### Judgement

Part 5 is useful as a controlled response task, but it only partially supports an Interaction score.

---

## 4. Construct Validity

The test includes tasks that elicit several aspects of spoken performance. Presentation and graphics presentation are particularly useful for assessing spoken production in workplace-like contexts.

The main construct validity concern is interaction. PROGOS reports an Interaction score, but the test provides limited opportunity to demonstrate the core features of interactional competence. The communication activity is simulated and fixed rather than genuinely dialogic.

The test therefore appears stronger as a measure of spoken production than as a measure of spoken interaction.

---

## 5. Reliability

The test design supports consistency in administration. Prompts, timing, and delivery are standardised. The absence of human interlocutors and human examiners removes some sources of variability.

However, consistency of administration is not the same as full score reliability. From the reviewed materials, it is not possible to determine:

- how stable scores are across repeated administrations;
- how equivalent different test forms are;
- how much measurement error is associated with reported levels;
- whether subscores are equally reliable;
- how the automated scoring system handles atypical accents, background noise, hesitations, repairs, or unexpected but valid responses.

The appropriate conclusion is not that PROGOS is unreliable. Rather, the reviewed materials do not allow a full independent judgement of score reliability. At the same time, several design features, especially standardisation, are likely to support administrative consistency.

---

## 6. Fairness and Accessibility

### Strengths

The test uses standardised administration and generally clear instructions. Several topics are familiar and workplace-relevant. The graphics task uses fictional countries, which helps avoid some forms of cultural bias.

### Concerns

Some tasks assume workplace experience, such as discussing colleagues or job interviews. This may disadvantage test takers with limited work experience.

The use of English-only instructions may also increase the difficulty for lower-proficiency learners. If the construct is spoken English ability, then complex instructions or audio-only instructions can introduce additional listening and comprehension demands.

## 7. Evaluation of Score Reports

Two candidate reports were reviewed. The reports are visually clear and easy to understand. They provide an overall level, six analytic category scores, and learning advice.

However, the content of the comments raises significant concerns.

### 7.1 Overall Assessment

The Overall Assessment comment in the reviewed reports primarily addresses vocabulary and expressions. It refers to the ability to use simple words, phrases, sentences, basic vocabulary, and expressions. It only lightly touches interaction and says little or nothing about:

- accuracy;
- fluency;
- coherence;
- phonology;
- task performance;
- communicative effectiveness.

As a result, the Overall Assessment does not read like a synthesis of all six analytic categories. It reads more like a general lexical and functional descriptor.

This matters because users may interpret the Overall Assessment as a holistic summary of the candidate's speaking ability. The wording does not fully support that interpretation.

### 7.2 Range

The Range comment in the reviewed A2 High report states that the test taker can use a wide range of language and express themselves clearly across general, work, and leisure topics.

This wording appears stronger than the official CEFR B1 descriptor for general linguistic range, which is more cautious and includes hesitation, circumlocution, repetition, and difficulty with formulation.

The issue is not merely a wording difference. Inflated descriptors can lead learners, teachers, or managers to overestimate the test taker's ability.

### 7.3 Accuracy

The Accuracy comment is more defensible. It refers to correct use of some simple structures and basic errors such as tense and subject-verb agreement. This is consistent with an A2-level description of grammatical control.

The related learning advice is also broadly relevant, although still generic.

### 7.4 Fluency

The Fluency comments vary by candidate level. One reviewed report gave Fluency as B2 and above, while the other gave Fluency as B1.

Some fluency advice is relevant, such as maintaining speech when speaking at length. However, some examples move away from fluency into pragmatic or discourse advice. For example, advice to practise using "Hmm" to express doubt or disagreement is not clearly fluency feedback. It is more closely related to interaction, pragmatics, or discourse management.

This suggests that the report categories and the learning advice are not always construct-aligned.

### 7.5 Interaction

The Interaction comment is a major concern.

The report states that the candidate can exchange information and interact on familiar everyday topics if the other person leads the conversation.

This is problematic because the test is fully automated and non-adaptive. The candidate has no opportunity to demonstrate whether they can lead a conversation, respond to an interlocutor's misunderstanding, negotiate meaning, ask for clarification, or manage turn-taking.

The report comment may be a reasonable CEFR-style descriptor, but it is not well supported by the observed task design.

The learning advice also shifts toward opinion-giving formulae such as "In my opinion" and "I guess that". These are useful phrases, but they do not directly address interactional competence in the fuller sense.

### 7.6 Coherence

The Coherence comment is broadly defensible. It describes connecting ideas into a linear sequence and the advice focuses on linking expressions such as "for example" and "for one thing".

However, the report still does not cite evidence from the individual's actual performance. It does not say whether the candidate organised the presentation well, omitted transitions, overused simple connectors, or failed to structure the graph response.

### 7.7 Phonology

The Phonology comment is broadly CEFR-like, referring to intelligibility, stress, intonation, and first-language influence.

However, the learning advice is quite general. Recommendations such as increasing exposure to English, watching TED Talks, or repeating speech extracts may be pedagogically useful, but they are not individualised pronunciation feedback. The report does not identify specific pronunciation features, stress problems, rhythm issues, or intelligibility breakdowns.

---

## 8. Evidence of Templating in Feedback

Comparison of two candidate reports suggests that the feedback is largely level-based and templated.

Many sections follow the same pattern:

1. a general descriptor;
2. level-up advice;
3. a concrete study example;
4. model sentences;
5. reference expressions or resources.

This format is easy to read and may be useful for learners. However, the comments do not cite individual evidence from the test taker's actual speech. They do not refer to specific responses, task performance, errors, successful strategies, or observed breakdowns.

The reports therefore appear to provide level-based learning guidance rather than individual diagnostic feedback.

This distinction is important. Level-based guidance may be helpful, but it should not be interpreted as a detailed analysis of the individual's speaking performance.

---

## 9. Privacy and Terms Documentation

The reviewed privacy-management and terms pages provide general information about personal information handling and service use.

From the perspective of an automated speaking test, the reviewed documents provided limited specific detail about:

- retention of voice recordings;
- handling of speech data;
- whether speech data are used for product development or model improvement;
- whether transcripts are generated;
- whether test takers can request review of a machine-generated score;
- what data are available to organisations using the service.

No conclusion is drawn here about legal compliance. The point is narrower: for a speaking test that records voice and uses automated scoring, greater transparency about speech data and score review would be desirable from an assessment-governance perspective.

## 10. Suitability for the Organisation's Assessment Purposes

The organisation already has its own speaking and listening comprehension test. That test assesses six items, each scored from 1.0 to 9.0 in 0.25 increments. The six scores are averaged. This allows fine-grained ranking of learner ability and supports decisions about class allocation, pre/post-training assessment, and course design.

The organisation uses its internal assessment for:

1. student class allocation;
2. pre- and post-training assessment;
3. course design, including identification of levels, individual strengths and weaknesses, training content, and teacher selection.

### 10.1 Student Class Allocation

PROGOS may provide a broad indication of speaking proficiency. However, the reviewed reports do not provide the same level of fine-grained differentiation as the organisation's internal 1.0 to 9.0 scale.

For class allocation, the key issue is not only the level label but the ability to make meaningful distinctions among learners who are close in ability. Corporate classes often need grouping decisions within a broad proficiency band. A broad CEFR-style result may not be sufficient for this purpose.

Recommendation: PROGOS should not replace the internal assessment for class allocation.

### 10.2 Pre- and Post-Training Assessment

PROGOS is not recommended for this purpose.

CEFR levels are broad proficiency bands. Movement from one CEFR level to another usually requires substantial learning time. Many corporate training programmes are much shorter and are designed to produce targeted improvements rather than full CEFR-level movement.

As a result, a learner may improve meaningfully during a course while the PROGOS overall level remains unchanged. This could create the false impression that no progress occurred.

The problem is compounded by the nature of the feedback. If the report comments are largely level-based and generic, they are unlikely to show clearly what changed between pre- and post-test performances.

Recommendation: PROGOS should not be used as the main pre/post-training assessment tool for short or medium-length corporate programmes.

### 10.3 Course Design

PROGOS is not recommended for course design.

Effective course design requires information about specific strengths and weaknesses. For example, a training provider may need to know whether a learner's main issues involve:

- grammatical control;
- response length;
- idea organisation;
- pronunciation intelligibility;
- listening comprehension;
- turn-taking;
- hesitation and repair;
- business register;
- task-specific performance.

The reviewed PROGOS reports do not provide this level of individual diagnosis. They provide general descriptors and general learning advice. They do not cite specific performance evidence or give detailed information that would support teacher selection or targeted syllabus design.

Recommendation: PROGOS should not be used as the basis for course design or teacher assignment.

## 11. Comparison with the Internal Assessment System

| Purpose | Internal assessment | PROGOS | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Student class allocation | Fine-grained scoring from 1.0 to 9.0 in 0.25 increments | Broad proficiency indication | Keep internal assessment |
| Pre/post-training assessment | Sensitive to smaller changes if rating is reliable | CEFR-style levels may not change after short training | Do not use PROGOS as main pre/post measure |
| Course design | Can identify specific strengths and weaknesses | Feedback appears generic and level-based | Do not use PROGOS for course design |
| Teacher selection | Can support targeted assignment if diagnostic notes are available | Insufficient individualised information | Do not use PROGOS for teacher selection |
| Learner motivation | Depends on internal reporting style | Clear, learner-friendly reports | Possible supplementary use only |

---

## 12. Overall Recommendations

1. Do not replace the organisation's internal assessment with PROGOS.
2. Do not use PROGOS as the main instrument for pre/post-training evaluation.
3. Do not use PROGOS as the basis for course design or teacher assignment.
4. Treat PROGOS results, if used, as broad indicative proficiency information only.
5. Continue using the internal assessment for fine-grained ranking, diagnosis, and instructional planning.
6. If PROGOS is used at all, accompany it with a clear explanation of its limitations, especially regarding interaction and diagnostic feedback.

---

## 13. Final Judgement

PROGOS is a practical automated speaking test with several useful task types and clear learner-facing reports. It is strongest as a convenient broad proficiency indicator.

However, the test and reports have important limitations for the organisation's purposes. The measurement of interaction is limited by the fully automated and non-adaptive format. The feedback appears largely level-based rather than individualised. The reports do not provide sufficient diagnostic detail for course design or teacher selection. CEFR-style levels are too broad to serve as the main measure of short-term training progress.

For these reasons, PROGOS should not be adopted as a replacement for the organisation's existing speaking and listening assessment system.
