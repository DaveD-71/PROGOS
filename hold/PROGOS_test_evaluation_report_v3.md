# Independent Evaluation of the PROGOS English Speaking Test (Version 3)

## Executive Summary

This report evaluates the PROGOS English Speaking Test as:

1. an assessment instrument;
2. a reporting system; and
3. a potential tool for organisational decision-making.

The review is based solely on materials examined during this project and does not make assumptions regarding unpublished technical documentation, psychometric studies, or internal scoring procedures.

PROGOS is a fully automated, machine-run, non-adaptive speaking test. It has several strengths, particularly standardisation, practicality, scalability, and the inclusion of several workplace-relevant speaking tasks.

However, the review identified significant concerns regarding:

- the measurement of interactional competence;
- the suitability of the reports for diagnostic purposes;
- the interpretation of broad proficiency levels for short-term training evaluation; and
- the usefulness of the test for instructional decision-making.

The overall conclusion is that PROGOS may have value as a supplementary external benchmark providing a broad indication of speaking proficiency. It should not replace the organisation's existing internal speaking assessment system.

---

# 1. Purpose and Intended Use

## 1.1 Observed Purpose

Based on the reviewed materials, PROGOS appears to be designed to:

- provide an indication of speaking proficiency;
- report a CEFR-aligned overall level;
- provide analytic scores in six categories.

## 1.2 Appropriate Uses

- Broad external benchmarking.
- General indication of speaking proficiency.
- Supplementary information for broad placement decisions.

## 1.3 Inappropriate Uses

- Fine-grained placement.
- Measurement of short-term learning gains.
- Diagnostic assessment.
- Course design.
- Teacher allocation.

---

# 2. Test Methodology

## 2.1 Overview

The reviewed materials indicate five task types:

1. Interview responses.
2. Reading aloud.
3. Presentation.
4. Graphics presentation.
5. Communication activity.

## 2.2 Evaluation of Tasks

### Interview
Potentially useful for eliciting spontaneous speech. Insufficient evidence was available for a detailed item analysis.

### Reading Aloud
Strengths:
- highly standardised;
- useful for pronunciation evidence.

Limitations:
- limited evidence of communicative speaking ability;
- several reviewed sentences were awkward or unnatural.

### Presentation
One of the strongest tasks.
Provides evidence of:
- fluency;
- coherence;
- range;
- sustained spoken production.

### Graphics Presentation
Strong business authenticity.
Provides evidence of:
- discourse organisation;
- explanation of information;
- descriptive language.

Also measures graph interpretation skills.

### Communication Activity
Provides opportunities to respond to prompts.
Does not provide genuine interaction because the system is:
- automated;
- fixed;
- non-adaptive.

# 3. Validity

## 3.1 Content Validity

The test samples several aspects of spoken production.

## 3.2 Construct Validity

The principal concern relates to Interaction.

The test provides limited opportunities to demonstrate:

- negotiation of meaning;
- repair;
- conversational initiative;
- turn-taking;
- co-construction of meaning.

The test therefore appears stronger as a measure of spoken production than spoken interaction.

## 3.3 Authenticity

Most tasks are reasonably authentic and workplace-relevant.

The Reading Aloud task is substantially less authentic.

## 3.4 Interpretive Validity

Some report comments appear broader than the evidence generated by the tasks themselves.

## 3.5 Consequential Validity

There is a risk that stakeholders may:

- overinterpret unchanged scores;
- underestimate genuine learning gains;
- treat generic feedback as individual diagnosis.

---

# 4. Reliability

The design supports consistency of administration through:

- standardised prompts;
- identical timing;
- automated delivery.

However, the reviewed materials do not permit conclusions regarding:

- score stability;
- measurement error;
- equivalence of forms;
- reliability of subscores.

Accordingly, no conclusion is reached regarding overall score reliability.

---

# 5. Test Instructions and Language

The language of instructions is an important consideration.

Potential concerns include:

- English-only instructions;
- audio-only instructions in some sections;
- additional listening and processing demands.

If the construct of interest is speaking ability, instructions should minimise construct-irrelevant difficulty.

The reviewed materials suggest that instructions may increase cognitive load for lower-level learners.

---

# 6. Fairness and Accessibility

## Strengths

- standardised administration;
- generally familiar topics;
- relatively low cultural bias.

## Concerns

Some tasks assume:

- workplace experience;
- familiarity with professional contexts.

This may disadvantage some test takers.

---

# 7. Practicality

Practicality is one of PROGOS's strongest features.

Strengths include:

- fully automated administration;
- scalability;
- rapid reporting;
- standardised delivery;
- ease of deployment.

---

# 8. Evaluation of the Score Reports

## 8.1 Report Structure

The reports are visually clear and easy to understand.

## 8.2 Overall Assessment Comment

The Overall Assessment comments primarily address vocabulary and expressions and do not fully reflect all six analytic categories.

## 8.3 Individual Categories

Some category comments appear broadly defensible.

The Interaction comments are problematic because the test provides only limited evidence of interactional competence.

## 8.4 Diagnostic Usefulness

The reports:

- do not cite evidence from individual performances;
- provide limited diagnostic information;
- provide largely generic recommendations.

## 8.5 Evidence of Templating

Comparison of reports suggests that the feedback is largely level-based and templated.

The reports therefore appear to provide:

> level-based learning guidance

rather than:

> individual diagnostic feedback.

---

# 9. Legal and Ethical Considerations

The reviewed documents provide general information regarding personal information handling and service use.

However, from an assessment-governance perspective, the reviewed materials provide limited information regarding:

- speech data handling;
- retention of recordings;
- score review procedures.

No conclusions regarding legal compliance are made in this report.

---

# 10. Suitability for the Organisation's Purposes

The organisation currently employs an internal assessment system that:

- assesses six dimensions;
- uses a 1.0–9.0 scale with 0.25 increments;
- supports placement, progress measurement, and course design.

## Class Allocation

PROGOS may provide supplementary information regarding broad proficiency.

It should not replace the internal assessment.

## Pre- and Post-Training Assessment

Not recommended.

CEFR-style proficiency bands are broad descriptors of ability.

Many corporate programmes are too short to produce movement between those bands.

Consequently, meaningful improvement may occur without any change in reported level.

## Course Design and Teacher Assignment

Not recommended.

The reports do not provide sufficient individual diagnostic information to support:

- syllabus design;
- teacher selection;
- targeted interventions.

---

# 11. Conclusions and Recommendations

| Purpose | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Broad external benchmarking | Possible supplementary use |
| Initial broad placement | Possible supplementary use |
| Fine-grained placement | Not recommended |
| Pre/post-training assessment | Not recommended |
| Course design | Not recommended |
| Teacher assignment | Not recommended |
| Diagnostic assessment | Not recommended |

---

# Final Judgement

PROGOS is a practical, scalable automated speaking test that provides a broad indication of speaking proficiency.

The principal limitation of PROGOS for this organisation is not that it is automated or CEFR-aligned. Rather, it is that the information produced by the test is insufficiently sensitive and insufficiently diagnostic for the purposes for which the organisation uses its internal assessment system.

Accordingly, PROGOS should not replace the organisation's existing speaking and listening assessment practices and should only be considered, if at all, as a supplementary external benchmark.
